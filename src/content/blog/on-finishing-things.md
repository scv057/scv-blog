title: 'On finishing things'
description: 'The graveyard of side projects is not a motivation problem. It''s a definition problem: nothing was ever small enough to be finishable.'

I used to think my abandoned side projects were a discipline problem. Start strong, drift, stall, feel bad, repeat. The standard advice — just push through — never worked, and I've come to believe it's because the diagnosis was wrong.

The projects didn't die from lack of willpower. They died because "done" was never defined. A project without a finish line doesn't get abandoned, exactly; it just never ends, and at some point you stop showing up to a race with no tape to break.

What changed things for me was shrinking the unit of done. Not "build a blog" but "get one page deployed today." Not "learn Rust" but "ship one ugly CLI tool." Each unit small enough that finishing is a matter of hours, not months — because finishing, it turns out, is a skill you have to practice, and you can't practice it on projects that take a year to reach the end.

The compound effect is real. Finished things, even tiny ones, generate the evidence that you finish things. Unfinished things generate the opposite evidence, at higher volume.

This blog exists because of that math.
